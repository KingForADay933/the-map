Financial Literacy Overview

Managing money is a skill, not something you're born knowing. Once you understand the basics, a lot of the stress around finances starts to loosen its grip.

What Financial Literacy Actually Means

Financial literacy is understanding money well enough to make good decisions about it. It covers how you earn it, spend it, save it, protect it, and grow it over time. You don't need a finance degree to be financially literate. You just need a working understanding of a few core concepts and the habits to apply them.

Most people struggle with money not because they don't make enough, but because nobody ever explained how it works.

The Foundation: Income vs. Expenses

Everything in personal finance starts with one simple equation:

The Core Equation

What you keep = What you earn − What you spend. That's it. Every financial goal (saving, investing, getting out of debt) starts with widening that gap.

Before you can do anything meaningful with your money, you need two numbers: your average monthly income and your average monthly expenses. Most people have a rough idea of one and almost no idea of the other.

Building a Safety Net First

Once you get caught up in the swing of adult life, saving money might not come naturally. It's a lot to manage rent, utilities, and transportation, all while living a lifestyle that makes you reasonably happy, and still have anything left over. But with intention, it's usually more possible than it feels.

The place to start isn't investing or paying off debt. Start with an emergency fund. Unexpected things happen to everyone: a car repair, a medical bill, a gap between jobs. Without savings to absorb those hits, one bad week can cost you months of progress.

Starting goal: $500–$1,000
Enough to cover most minor emergencies without going into debt. This is your first target, even if you can only save $25 a week to get there.
Long-term goal: 3–6 months of expenses
If you lost your income tomorrow, this is how long you could survive without panic. Calculate your monthly essential expenses and multiply by 3 to 6.

Where to Go From Here

Financial literacy builds in layers. You don't need to master everything at once, just tackle things in the right order.

01

Know your numbers

Track your income and expenses for one month. Your bank app or statements make this easy. You can't manage what you can't see.

02

Build a budget

Once you know your numbers, set a plan for them. A budget doesn't restrict your life. It gives you permission to spend on what matters because you've already handled what doesn't.

03

Build your emergency fund

Before anything else, save a basic safety net. Park it in a high-yield savings account where it earns a little interest while it waits.

04

Tackle debt strategically

Not all debt is the same. High-interest debt (credit cards, payday loans) destroys wealth. Low-interest debt is less urgent. Know which is which.

05

Start investing, even a little

Once your foundation is stable, money sitting idle is slowly losing value to inflation. Start with your employer's 401(k) match, then a Roth IRA.
