Cooking 101

Cooking at home is one of the best financial decisions you can make as a young adult. It saves money, improves your health, and becomes genuinely satisfying once you get the basics down.

4 min read
TL;DR
  • Four techniques cover most everyday cooking: sautéing, boiling/simmering, roasting, and seasoning well.
  • Plan meals before you shop. Impulse buying is expensive. Store brands are almost always identical quality at significantly lower cost.
  • Frozen vegetables are not second-best, they're often more nutritious than fresh and far cheaper.
  • Cook extra. Eating leftovers the next day cuts the time cost per meal in half.
  • Five recipes to start: scrambled eggs with vegetables, spaghetti with ground beef, black bean rice bowl, egg fried rice, chicken tacos.

What You Actually Need to Start

You don't need a fully stocked kitchen. These basics handle the vast majority of everyday meals:

  • Chef's knife and cutting board: the most used tools in any kitchen
  • Large skillet or frying pan: nonstick is the most forgiving for beginners
  • Pot with a lid: for pasta, rice, soups, and anything boiled
  • Baking sheet: for roasting vegetables or anything going in the oven
  • Wooden spoon, spatula, measuring cups and spoons

A note on knives: learn basic knife safety before you start. Keep it sharp. A dull knife requires more force and slips more easily. Curl your fingers inward when chopping and let the weight of the knife do the work.

Four Techniques Worth Learning First

01

Sauteing

Cooking food quickly in a small amount of oil over medium-high heat. This is how you cook most vegetables, eggs, and thin cuts of meat. Get the pan hot before adding oil, and the oil hot before adding food.

02

Boiling and simmering

Boiling is full rolling bubbles. Simmering is gentle, small bubbles. Pasta goes in boiling water. Soups and sauces usually simmer. Most beginners boil when they should simmer and end up with mushy results.

03

Roasting

Putting food in the oven at high heat (400 to 425F) to develop deep, caramelized flavor. Works beautifully on almost any vegetable. Toss with olive oil, salt, and pepper, spread in a single layer, and don't overcrowd the pan.

04

Seasoning

Salt, pepper, garlic powder, and paprika can transform a plain ingredient into something that tastes intentional. Taste as you cook. You can always add more salt; you can't take it out.

Grocery Shopping on a Budget

  • Plan before you shop. Pick 3 to 5 meals for the week, write down what you need, and check what you already have. Impulse shopping is expensive.
  • Buy store brands. For staples like canned goods, pasta, and spices, store brands are almost always identical in quality and significantly cheaper.
  • Buy in bulk for staples. Rice, dried beans, oats, and spices are dramatically cheaper per serving when bought in larger quantities.
  • Skip pre-cut and pre-packaged. You pay a steep premium for convenience. A whole head of broccoli costs a fraction of pre-cut florets.
  • Frozen vegetables are not second-best. They're picked and frozen at peak ripeness, often more nutritious than fresh produce that's been sitting in transit, and cheaper.

Versatile staples to always have

Rice and pasta
Cheap, filling, and the base of dozens of easy meals. Keep both on hand.
Eggs
One of the most affordable, protein-dense foods available. Breakfast, fried rice, sandwiches, frittatas.
Canned beans and tomatoes
Long shelf life, high protein and fiber, incredibly versatile. Black beans, chickpeas, diced tomatoes.
Frozen vegetables
No prep, no waste, no spoiling. Add to anything.
Onions and garlic
They add flavor to almost everything and cost almost nothing. Almost every savory recipe starts with one or both.

5 Recipes to Start With

1. Scrambled Eggs with Vegetables

Ingredients: 2 eggs, a handful of spinach or bell peppers, salt, pepper, optional cheese.

Instructions: Heat a small amount of oil in a pan over medium heat. Add vegetables and cook 2 to 3 minutes. Beat eggs in a bowl, pour into the pan, and stir gently until just set. Season and serve.

2. Spaghetti with Ground Beef

Ingredients: 8 oz spaghetti, half a pound ground beef, 1 can diced tomatoes or a jar of pasta sauce, 1 clove garlic (minced), Italian seasoning, salt and pepper.

Instructions: Cook spaghetti according to package directions. In a separate pan over medium-high heat, brown the ground beef, breaking it apart as it cooks. Drain excess fat. Add garlic and cook 1 minute. Add tomatoes or sauce and simmer 5 minutes. Season with Italian seasoning, salt, and pepper. Serve over pasta.

3. Black Bean and Rice Bowl

Ingredients: 1 cup cooked rice, half a can of black beans (drained and rinsed), a quarter cup of salsa, optional shredded cheese.

Instructions: Heat the beans in a pot or microwave. Layer rice, beans, and salsa in a bowl. Add cheese if using. Done in under 10 minutes.

4. Egg Fried Rice

Ingredients: 2 cups cooked rice (day-old works best), 2 eggs, 1 cup frozen mixed vegetables, 2 tablespoons soy sauce, half a teaspoon garlic powder, 1 tablespoon oil.

Instructions: Heat oil in a pan over medium-high heat. Add frozen vegetables and cook 3 minutes. Push to one side, crack in the eggs, and scramble until just set. Add the rice and mix everything together. Pour in soy sauce and garlic powder, stir to combine, and cook another 2 minutes until the rice is heated through.

5. Chicken Tacos

Ingredients: 2 chicken breasts or thighs, 1 teaspoon each cumin and garlic powder, half a teaspoon chili powder, salt and pepper, small tortillas, toppings of your choice (shredded cheese, salsa, lettuce).

Instructions: Season the chicken on both sides with cumin, garlic powder, chili powder, salt, and pepper. Heat oil in a pan over medium heat and cook chicken 5 to 6 minutes per side, until cooked through to 165°F internally. Let it rest 5 minutes, then slice or shred. Warm the tortillas, fill with chicken, and add toppings.

Cook extra

Cooking extra and eating leftovers the next day is one of the most effective habits in home cooking. It cuts the time cost per meal in half and removes the temptation to order out when you're tired.
